OUR ORIGIN

A Whiff of Whimsy

For forty years, Captain Barnaby Higgins sailed the choppy waters of the Gulf of Maine, chasing lobsters and dodging nor'easters. But it was on his daily shore walks along the rocky hemlock-lined coves of Boothbay Harbor that he found his true calling.

It started with a single, perfectly sun-bleached skunk cranium wedged between two granite ledges. Impressed by the density and pearlescent sheen of the bone—a result of the local skunk population's high-calcium diet of lobster shells and soft-shell clams—Barnaby brought it home, cleaned it, and set it on his mantle.

"A lobster shell feeds a skunk. The Maine wind bleaches the bone. I just do the dusting." — Captain Barnaby

Word spread from Edgecomb to Wiscasset. Summer folks wanted them. Locals wanted them for good luck during winter gales. Thus, SkunkBones.com was born. Today, Barnaby operates a gentle, non-invasive salvage operation. Working in tandem with local woodsmen and landowners, we collect bones only from animals that have naturally passed. It’s a sustainable, circular economy that pays homage to Maine’s most misunderstood mammal.

Our Deodorization Process

Many ask, "Does it... smell?" The answer is a resounding no. Each bone undergoes the proprietary Boothbay Triple-Wash: a 14-day bath in distilled sea-mist, baking soda, and wild Maine rosemary, followed by 30 days of sun-bleaching on high racks overlooking the Atlantic gales. The result is a pure, chalk-white, completely odorless calcium specimen.

State Laws & Shipping Disclosures

We believe in absolute transparency, ethical custody, and avoiding game warden audits.

Is this actually legal?

Yes, in the State of Maine. Under the Maine Department of Inland Fisheries & Wildlife regulations, the Striped Skunk (Mephitis mephitis) is classified as a furbearer. Naturally salvaged skeletal remains of furbearing animals that died of natural causes or accidents may be legally collected, possessed, and sold commercially by licensed residents. We hold all necessary local salvage permits and state registrations.

100% Maine Legal

State-by-State Shipping Exclusions

Wildlife laws vary dramatically across the United States. Several states have strict regulations prohibiting the commercial sale or import of wild furbearer parts, or require complex commercial licenses. To ensure compliance, we cannot ship any bone specimens to the following states:

  • California: Strict regulations on wild animal parts import (Cal. Fish & Game Code § 3039).
  • Connecticut: Strict regulations on wild mammal parts commercial trade (Conn. Gen. Stat. § 26-78).
  • New York: Restricts the sale of parts of wild furbearing animals without local dealer permits.
  • Texas: Restricts trade in native wild furbearers and their parts.
  • Georgia: Prohibits import/sale of wild-caught mammal parts.
  • Hawaii: Heavy bio-security and import restrictions on animal materials.
  • Washington: Restricted commercial trade of native wildlife skeletons.

If you place an order with a shipping address in one of these states, our checkout validator will block the order. We apologize to skeletal enthusiasts in these regions.

Frequently Legal Questions

Do you ship internationally?

No. Under the federal Lacey Act and various international treaty regulations (including CITES guidelines on mammalian exports), international shipment of wildlife bones is extremely complex. We ship exclusively to eligible U.S. states.

Does a certificate of origin come with my purchase?

Yes. Every bone or set comes with a hand-stamped Certificate of Ethical Salvage, detailing the general recovery area (e.g., "Edgecomb Ridge", "Southport Marsh") and the year of salvage, signed by Captain Barnaby Higgins.

Skunk Skeletal Trivia

Impress your dinner party guests with these unique biological facts about the Striped Skunk (Mephitis mephitis).

Interactive Skeletal Map

1. The High-Calcium Lobster Diet

Midcoast Maine skunks frequent lobster traps, wharves, and trash bins, eating high volumes of lobster carapace. This rich marine calcium makes their bones roughly 14% denser and shinier than inland skunks.

2. Scent-Resistant Calcium Matrix

While a skunk's scent glands are highly potent, their skeletal structure does not store musk. The bones are standard, high-purity mammalian calcium and contain zero scent compounds once properly cured.

3. 180-Degree Spine Flexibility

A skunk's lumbar vertebrae are uniquely spaced, allowing the animal to bend its spine in an extreme U-shape. This permits them to face their attacker and aim their scent glands simultaneously.

4. The Sagittal Crest

Mature male skunks have a distinct raised ridge along the top of their skull (the sagittal crest). This anchor point supports incredibly strong jaw muscles, useful for cracking crab shells and wild acorns.
